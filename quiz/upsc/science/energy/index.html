Question 4hard

The unit of radioactivity is:

  1. Becquerel (Bq) — 1 Bq = 1 disintegration per second; older unit: Curie (1 Ci = 3.7×10¹⁰ Bq)
  2. Gray
  3. Sievert
  4. Roentgen
Show answer

Answer: Becquerel (Bq) — 1 Bq = 1 disintegration per second; older unit: Curie (1 Ci = 3.7×10¹⁰ Bq)

Radioactivity units: Becquerel (Bq) = 1 nuclear decay/second (SI unit). Curie (Ci) = 3.7×10¹⁰ Bq (older unit). Gray (Gy) = absorbed dose of radiation (1 J/kg). Sievert (Sv) = effective dose (accounts for radiation type's biological harm). Dose in Sieverts matters for radiation protection.

Question 5medium

The half-life of a radioactive substance is:

  1. Time for exactly half of the radioactive atoms to decay — constant for each isotope (C-14: 5730 years, U-235: 703 million years)
  2. Time for all atoms to decay
  3. Time for substance to double
  4. Temperature-dependent
Show answer

Answer: Time for exactly half of the radioactive atoms to decay — constant for each isotope (C-14: 5730 years, U-235: 703 million years)

Half-life (t½): time for half the radioactive nuclei to decay. It is constant for each radioisotope regardless of amount or conditions. After n half-lives, amount remaining = (½)ⁿ × initial amount. Used in carbon dating (C-14, t½=5,730 y), nuclear medicine (Tc-99m, t½=6 h), and nuclear fuel assessment.
