Question 2easy

The law of demand states that, all else equal:

  1. When price of a good rises, quantity demanded falls (inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded)
  2. When price rises, demand rises
  3. Demand is always constant
  4. Supply determines demand
Show answer

Answer: When price of a good rises, quantity demanded falls (inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded)

Law of Demand (Alfred Marshall): price and quantity demanded have an inverse relationship — as price rises, quantity demanded falls (consumers buy less; some switch to substitutes). Exceptions (Giffen goods, Veblen goods, necessities, wartime hoarding). Demand curve slopes downward.

  3. Supply is constant regardless of price
  4. Demand determines supply

Answer: When price of a good rises, quantity supplied increases (direct/positive relationship between price and quantity supplied)

Law of Supply: price and quantity supplied have a direct relationship — as price rises, producers are willing to supply more (higher profit incentive). Supply curve slopes upward. Exceptions: agricultural products (time lag), antiques/art (fixed supply). Market equilibrium: demand = supply.

Question 6hard

The 'Dutch Flower Auction' pricing mechanism is used for:

  1. Price starting high and descending until a buyer accepts — also called 'descending auction' or 'clock auction'; used for flowers, fish, tobacco, and some treasury securities
  2. Price starting low and going up (English auction)
  3. Fixed price by government
  4. Price set by producer only
Show answer

Answer: Price starting high and descending until a buyer accepts — also called 'descending auction' or 'clock auction'; used for flowers, fish, tobacco, and some treasury securities

Dutch (Descending) Auction: auctioneer starts at high price, lowers until a buyer bids. First bidder who accepts becomes the buyer. Advantage: fast (one round). Used in: Dutch flower market (hence name), fish markets, US Treasury auctions (actually uniform price), Google's IPO. Contrasts with English (ascending) auction where price rises until one bidder remains.

Question 14medium

The 'Phillips Curve' in economics (A.W. Phillips, 1958) shows:

  1. Inverse relationship between unemployment rate and inflation rate — when unemployment is low (economy is booming), wages/prices rise faster; when unemployment is high, inflation is low
  2. Direct relationship between unemployment and inflation
  3. No relationship between unemployment and inflation
  4. Relationship between GDP and inflation only
Show answer

Answer: Inverse relationship between unemployment rate and inflation rate — when unemployment is low (economy is booming), wages/prices rise faster; when unemployment is high, inflation is low

Phillips Curve (1958): negative correlation between unemployment and wage inflation in UK data 1861-1957. Policy implication: policymakers can trade-off (accept more inflation for less unemployment). Stagflation of 1970s disproved simple Phillips Curve. Long-run Phillips Curve (Friedman-Phelps): vertical at Natural Rate of Unemployment (NAIRU) — no long-run trade-off.
