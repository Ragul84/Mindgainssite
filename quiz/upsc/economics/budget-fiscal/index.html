The Marginal Propensity to Consume (MPC) is:

  1. Fraction of additional income spent on consumption (ΔC/ΔY); MPC + MPS (Marginal Propensity to Save) = 1; if MPC = 0.8, 80 paise of each extra rupee of income is consumed
  2. Total consumption divided by total income
  3. Savings rate
  4. Fixed cost ratio
Show answer

Answer: Fraction of additional income spent on consumption (ΔC/ΔY); MPC + MPS (Marginal Propensity to Save) = 1; if MPC = 0.8, 80 paise of each extra rupee of income is consumed

MPC = ΔC/ΔY (change in consumption / change in income). MPS = ΔS/ΔY. MPC + MPS = 1. In Keynesian economics, MPC determines the fiscal multiplier: k = 1/(1-MPC) = 1/MPS. Higher MPC → larger multiplier → larger impact of government spending on income. Poor people have higher MPC (spend more of extra income) than rich (who save more).

Question 22medium

Price elasticity of demand (PED) measures:

  1. Percentage change in quantity demanded / Percentage change in price — elastic: |PED|>1 (luxury goods, many substitutes); inelastic: |PED|<1 (necessities, few substitutes like salt, insulin)
  2. Total demand
  3. Consumer income levels
  4. Production capacity
Show answer

Answer: Percentage change in quantity demanded / Percentage change in price — elastic: |PED|>1 (luxury goods, many substitutes); inelastic: |PED|<1 (necessities, few substitutes like salt, insulin)

PED = %ΔQd / %ΔP. Elastic (|PED|>1): consumer responds strongly to price changes — luxury goods (gold jewelry, cars). Key for tax policy: taxing inelastic goods (petrol, tobacco) raises more revenue with less market distortion.
